While getting a "24-hour urine" and measuring how quickly the patient excretes creatinine would be optimal, it has one major drawback: It takes 24 hours. We need methods for estimating the patient's glomerular filtration rate quickly.

Cockroft and Gault developed an equation that estimates GFR based on the patient's

  • Serum creatinine
  • Body height/weight
  • Age
  • Gender

While it may not be the most accurate equation for estimating GFR, it can be done using paper and pencil (no calculator needed). For most situations involving drug dosing, it is close enough.

Looking back at our question, we see that it is asking for a glomerular filtration rate. That's a measure of how fast the kidneys can filter blood. The usual way of expressing GFR is mL/minute. When directly measuring creatinine clearance using a 24-hour urine, we would cross out units in order to end up with mL/min. However, units don't cross out when using the Cockroft-Gault equation. Rather, we have to make sure we are using the same units that they did when they created the equation. We have to express

  • Serum creatinine in mg/dL
  • Weight in kg
  • Age in years
If we don't use those units, our number will not come out right.

We know that a 'typical' GFR is usually about 100 mL/min. Some healthy, younger patients might have calculated GFRs of 125-150 mL/min. A person in renal failure might have a GFR of less than 10 mL/min.

Our answer is probably going to be somewhere between 0 - 150 mL/min.

Obviously, we will use the Cockroft-Gault equation.

However, we need to think about how we are going to interpret the values we are going to enter into the equation.

.

Let's start with age. Intuitively, it makes sense that as age increases, GFR decreases.

The thing to keep in mind, though, is that Cockroft and Gault developed their equation using data collected from patients whom we would consider "young" or "middle-aged" adults. They didn't use data from many patients who were 70, 80 or 90 years old.

.

Let's think about the situation where we have a 90 year old man who weighs 50 kg with a serum creatinine of 0.4 mg/dL. That's a low serum creatinine. Is his serum creatinine low because he has super-performing kidneys?

Of course not. He has a very low creatinine because creatinine comes from muscle. He doesn't have as much muscle mass as a man who is 35 years old. So, he won't be making as much creatinine in the first place.

.

Some sources recommend ignoring the elderly patient's low actual serum creatinine and instead using 1.0 mg/dL in the Cockroft-Gault equation for such a patient. The idea is that since the patient's serum creatinine is "falsely low", arbitrarily increasing the creatinine will give us a closer estimate of the patient's actual GFR.

However, recent studies have suggested that this practice significantly underestimates the patient's GFR. A position paper published by ACCP calls rounding the serum creatinine to 1.0 mg/dL in elderly patients "common, [but] is not evidence-based".

So, what does one do? Probably the best solution when dealing with such a patient is to collect urine as soon as possible and directly measure the patient's creatinine clearance. Until that result comes back, dosing of drugs should use the elderly patient's actual serum creatinine in the Cockcroft-Gault equation. Then, the clinician should ask themselves:

  • How toxic is the drug that I am giving?
  • How sick is the patient?
  • Which would do more harm: Overdosing the patient or undertreating the disease?

The answers to such questions are, of course, beyond the scope of this tutorial.

The other extreme would be children. The Cockcroft-Gault equation was not developed for use in children and should not be used. There are other methods of estimating GFR in pediatric patients.

 

.

The next thing to think about is weight. Creatinine is produced by muscle, not by adipose tissue (fat). Looking at the Cockroft-Gault equation, it should be clear that using an obese patient's body weight would give a higher estimate the patient's GFR than that of an identical patient who was at their ideal body weight. It doesn't really make sense to think that someone who is grossly obese would have better functioning kidneys. Someone who is three times larger due to adipose tissue doesn't have three times larger kidneys.

So, the question is "What should we put in for weight when the patient is obese?" When the patient has BMI > 30 kg/m2, the best estimates of GFR seem to come from using a weight that reflects an obese person's muscle mass rather than their actual body weight.

.

There have been several strategies proposed for trying to estimate this "lean" body weight. This equation has been used most often to estimate "ideal body weight" in men.

The way this equation is used is that one subtracts 5 feet (60 inches) from the patient's height. That number is multipled times 2.3. Then 50 kg is added.

In this case, the terms "ideal body weight" and "lean body weight" are being used inter-changeably. There really isn't anything "ideal" about IBW and a person who is above their IBW is not necessarily obese. "Lean body weight" is probably a better name. However, most textbooks and articles refer to that equation as "IBW", so that is what will be used here.

There are also other ways of estimating "lean body weight", some of which might be more appropriate for certain drugs.

If the patient is underweight (actual weight < IBW), one would use their actual body weight in the Cockcroft-Gault equation. One would not add kilograms that don't exist.

Serum creatinine is inversely proportional to GFR. The lower the patient's glomerular filtration rate, the more creatinine accumulates in the serum.

Confidence of the the estimate is decreased if the patient is elderly, obese or has rapidly changing renal function.
