While getting a "24-hour urine" and measuring how quickly the patient excretes creatinine would be optimal, it has one major drawback: It takes 24 hours. We need methods for estimating the patient's glomerular filtration rate quickly.

Cockroft and Gault developed an equation that estimates GFR based on the patient's

  • Serum creatinine
  • Body height/weight
  • Age
  • Gender

While it may not be the most accurate equation for estimating GFR, it can be done using paper and pencil (no calculator needed). For most situations involving drug dosing, it is close enough.

Looking back at our question, we see that it is asking for a glomerular filtration rate. That's a measure of how fast the kidneys can filter blood. The usual way of expressing GFR is mL/minute. When directly measuring creatinine clearance using a 24-hour urine, we would cross out units in order to end up with mL/min. However, units don't cross out when using the Cockroft-Gault equation. Rather, we have to make sure we are using the same units that they did when they created the equation. We have to express

  • Serum creatinine in mg/dL
  • Weight in kg
  • Age in years
If we don't use those units, our number will not come out right.

We know that a 'typical' GFR is usually about 100 mL/min. Some healthy, younger patients might have calculated GFRs of 125-150 mL/min. A person in renal failure might have a GFR of less than 10 mL/min.

Our answer is probably going to be somewhere between 0 - 150 mL/min.

Obviously, we will use the Cockroft-Gault equation.

However, we need to think about how we are going to interpret the values we are going to enter into the equation.

.

Let's start with age. Intuitively, it makes sense that as age increases, GFR decreases.

The thing to keep in mind, though, is that Cockroft and Gault developed their equation using data collected from patients whom we would consider "young" or "middle-aged" adults. They didn't use data from many patients who were 70, 80 or 90 years old.

.

Let's think about the situation where we have a 90 year old man with a serum creatinine of 0.4 mg/dL. That's a very low serum creatinine. Is his serum creatinine low because he has super-performing kidneys?

Of course not. He has a very low creatinine because creatinine comes from muscle. He doesn't have as much muscle mass as a man who is 35 years old.

.

Some sources recommend ignoring the patient's actual serum creatinine and instead using 1.0 mg/dL in the Cockroft-Gault equation for such a patient. The idea is that this will keep us from over-estimating the patient's GFR.

However, recent studies have suggested that this practice significantly underestimates the patient's GFR. A position paper published by ACCP calls rounding the serum creatinine to 1.0 mg/dL in elderly patients "common, [but] is not evidence-based".

So, what does one do? Probably the best solution is to collect urine as soon as possible and directly measure the patient's creatinine clearance. Until that result comes back, dosing of drugs should use the elderly patient's actual serum creatinine in the Cockcroft-Gault equation. Then, the clinician should ask themselves:

  • How toxic is the drug that I am giving?
  • How sick is the patient?
  • Which would do more harm: Overdosing the patient or undertreating the disease?

The answers to such questions are, of course, beyond the scope of this tutorial.

.

The other extreme would be children. The Cockcroft-Gault equation was not developed for use in children and should not be used. There are other methods of estimating GFR in pediatric patients.

 

.

The next thing to think about is weight. Creatinine is produced by muscle, not by adipose tissue (fat). Looking at the Cockroft-Gault equation, it should be clear that using an obese patient's actual body weight would give a higher estimate the patient's GFR. It doesn't really make sense to think that someone who is grossly obese would have better functioning kidneys than an identical patient who was at their ideal body weight.

.

So, the question is "What should we put in for weight when the patient is obese?" The best estimates of GFR seem to come from using a weight that reflects an obese person's muscle mass rather than their actual body weight.

The hardest part of using this equation is making sure that the units cross out correctly. Always double check to see how the lab is reporting concentrations. They may not always be using the same units as in this tutorial. That might require you to first convert between mL, dL, mg or grams. The units for urine and blood concentrations need to be the same so that they cross out.

.

Once we get the units of concentration to cancel out correctly, what left is volume over time. We previously said we expected our answer to look like this, so that's a good sign.

This test is sometimes referred to as a "24-hour urine". That's because we often collect urine over a 24-hour period. However, there's nothing magical about "24 hours". The collection period can be any length of time. Collecting urine over 24 hours will probably give us a more accurate estimate of GFR than collecting over just 1-2 hours. But a 6-hour, 12-hour, or 36-hour urine collection would be calculated the same way. In our current case, the urine was collected over {{Problem.durationUrineCollection | number:0}} hours.

While this is one of the more accurate ways of estimating GFR, it has a big drawback: We first collected urine for {{Problem.durationUrineCollection | number:0}} hours. If a patient comes in and needs drug therapy, we want to figure out what dose to given them now, not in {{Problem.durationUrineCollection | number:0}} hours.

And while we could have used a shorter urine collection period, that reduces the accuracy of our result.

That's why this technique is used much less frequently than using equations that estimate GFR based on population averages. Using Cockcroft-Gault or MDRD you can estimte GFR using just a blood sample. Neither technique is as good as getting a timed urine collection, but the estimate is usually good enough to figure out a drug dose.
