Looking back at our question, we see that it is asking for a glomerular filtration rate. That's a measure of how fast the kidneys can filter blood. The usual way of expressing GFR is mL/minute. When directly measuring creatinine clearance using a 24-hour urine, we would cross out units in order to end up with mL/min. However, units don't cross out when using the MDRD equation. Rather, we have to make sure we are using the same units that they did when they created the equation. We have to express

  • Serum creatinine in mg/dL
  • BUN in mg/dL
  • Serum albumin in g/dL
  • Weight in kg
  • Age in years
If we don't use those units, our number will not come out right.

We know that a 'typical' GFR is usually about 100 mL/min. Some healthy, younger patients might have calculated GFRs of 125-150 mL/min. A person in renal failure might have a GFR of less than 10 mL/min.

Our answer is probably going to be somewhere between 0 - 150 mL/min.

Obviously, we will use the MDRD equation.

The question that may not be obvious is which MDRD equation will we use? There are at least four different versions and one "reexpression" .

This is the original "six-factor" version that was published in 1999.

The "four-factor" version below dropped the use of albumin and BUN. It was found to be as accurate as the original "six-factor" version and was recommended by the National Kidney Foundation in 2002.

Following an effort to standardize the way that laboratories were reporting serum creatinine values, a reexpression of the four-factor equation was published. An opinion paper published by ACCP in 2011 recommends that this version be used.

One thing to keep in mind is that this is an estimate of measured creatinine clearance, which is itself an estimate of GFR. Confidence of the the estimate is decreased if the patient is elderly, obese or has rapidly changing renal function. So, if the patient is being treated with a drug with a narrow therapeutic index or is critically ill, a 24-hour urine collection to measure actual creatinine clearance would be advisable.
