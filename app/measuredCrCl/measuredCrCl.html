Step 1: Draw a picture.

Creatinine is a waste product created by the natural turnover of muscle cells. It is almost entirely eliminated via glomerular filtration. Very little of it undergoes active secretion into the renal tubule or is reabsorbed from the tubule. Thus, seeing how quickly it gets into the urine can give us a good estimate of the kidney's ability to filter blood.

.
.

In this process, we are looking at

  • How much creatinine is in the blood
  • How much of that creatinine comes out in the urine over time

So, what we are going to do is collect urine over a period of time. We will also collect a blood sample. Ideally, the blood sample would be at the midpoint of the urine collection. However, serum creatinine doesn't change that much over a period of a few hours, so the timing of the blood sample is not critical.

After the urine collection is complete, the blood and the urine are sent to the lab. The lab will measure how much creatinine there is in the two samples.

If the kidneys are working well, a larger amount of creatinine should end up in the urine. However, if the kidneys are not functioning, very little creatinine will end up in the urine.

Looking back at our question, we see that it is asking for a glomerular filtration rate. That's a measure of how fast the kidneys can filter blood. The usual way of expressing GFR is mL/minute. If we cross out units and don't end up with mL/min, we've done something wrong.

We also know that a 'typical' GFR is usually about 100 mL/min. Some healthy, younger patients might have calculated GFRs of 125-150 mL/min. A person in renal failure might have a GFR of less than 10 mL/min.

Our answer is probably going to be somewhere between 0 - 150 mL/min.

The hardest part of using this equation is making sure that the units cross out correctly. Always double check to see how the lab is reporting concentrations. They may not always be using the same units as in this tutorial. That might require you to first convert between mL, dL, mg or grams. The units for urine and blood concentrations need to be the same so that they cross out.

.

Once we get the units of concentration to cancel out correctly, what left is volume over time. We previously said we expected our answer to look like this, so that's a good sign.

We can now start working on time. Note that we expect our answer to have minutes in the denominator. We need to make sure that's what we use.

This test is sometimes referred to as a "24-hour urine". That's because we often collect urine over a 24-hour period. However, there's nothing magical about "24 hours". The collection period can be any length of time. Collecting urine over 24 hours will probably give us a more accurate estimate of GFR than collecting over just 1-2 hours. But a 6-hour, 12-hour, or 36-hour urine collection would be calculated the same way. In our current case, the urine was collected over {{Problem.durationUrineCollection | number:0}} hours.

While this is one of the more accurate ways of estimating GFR, it has a big drawback: We first collected urine for {{Problem.durationUrineCollection | number:0}} hours. If a patient comes in and needs drug therapy, we want to figure out what dose to given them now, not in {{Problem.durationUrineCollection | number:0}} hours.

And while we could have used a shorter urine collection period, that reduces the accuracy of our result.

That's why this technique is used much less frequently than using equations that estimate GFR based on population averages. Using Cockcroft-Gault or MDRD you can estimte GFR using just a blood sample. Neither technique is as good as getting a timed urine collection, but the estimate is usually good enough to figure out a drug dose.
