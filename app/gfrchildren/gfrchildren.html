Looking back at our question, we see that it is asking for a glomerular filtration rate. That's a measure of how fast the kidneys can filter blood. The usual way of expressing GFR is mL/minute. When directly measuring creatinine clearance using a 24-hour urine, we would cross out units in order to end up with mL/min. However, units don't cross out when using the Schwartz equation. Rather, we have to make sure we are using the same units that they did when they created the equation. We have to express

  • Serum creatinine in mg/dL
  • Height in centimeters
If we don't use those units, our number will not come out right.

We know that a 'typical' GFR is usually about 100 mL/min. Some healthy, younger patients might have calculated GFRs of 125-150 mL/min. A person in renal failure might have a GFR of less than 10 mL/min.

Our answer is probably going to be somewhere between 0 - 150 mL/min.

We will use the Schwartz equation.

"Age" does not appear in the equation. Instead, age is reflected in the selection of the value for "k".

  • For patients between 1 and ~16 years of age, k = 0.413
  • For full-term infants under 1 year of age, k = 0.45
  • For pre-term infants, k = 0.33

Prior to the publication of an updated equation in 2009, k=0.55 was commonly used for children greater than 1 year of age.
